Why 3? Because an oscillator (anything that vibrates back and forth) has exactly three states: moving one way (+), sitting still (0), and moving the other way (−). Three independent oscillation axes give three dimensions of space. That is not a choice — it is the definition of oscillation.

From d = 3, **every other number in physics is derived by pure geometry and math.** No other numbers are put in by hand.

## The Stage: A Cubic Lattice

Picture space as a 3D grid of tiny connected springs — a cubic lattice. Each connection point can wiggle, and the wiggles spread as waves. The spacing of the grid is the Planck length (about 10⁻³⁵ metres — a billion billion times smaller than a proton).

The rule book for how the springs move is written in one equation called the **Lagrangian**:

> L = ½(stretch)² + (1/π²)(1 − cos(π × displacement))

That is it — one equation, zero adjustable knobs. The cosine term means the springs are nonlinear (like a guitar string that gets stiffer the harder you pluck it). This nonlinearity is where all the interesting physics comes from.

A key property: the spring stiffness and the inertia of the medium are exactly equal (both equal 2/π). This means waves travel without any reflection or loss. That is **why** the speed of light is the same for everyone — it is a fixed property of the medium, not a postulate.

## What Is a "Particle"?

When you pluck a guitar string fixed at both ends, only certain frequencies ring clearly — standing waves. Everything else fades away.

A particle is a standing wave on the lattice. Different stable patterns give different particles. The lattice has room for exactly **24 standing wave modes** — matching the 24 proper rotations of a cube. These 24 modes account for all the known fermions (quarks and leptons) of the Standard Model.

**The proton** is a large, tightly wound wave — like a smoke ring (a toroidal vortex) that wraps around the lattice and excites many modes at once. The **electron** is a smaller, simpler oscillation confined to a single site.

Their mass ratio? It comes from counting how many lattice modes the proton excites compared to the electron:

> m_proton / m_electron = 6 × π⁵ × (1 + tiny correction) = **1836.153**

The observed value is 1836.153. That is accurate to better than one part per billion — and nothing was adjusted to make it work.

## Where Do the Forces Come From?

Forces are just waves pushing and pulling on each other through the lattice.

**Gravity:** On a 3D lattice, any push between two points splits into two parts — 1/3 along the line connecting them (longitudinal) and 2/3 sideways (transverse). The longitudinal 1/3 always pulls inward. That is gravity. It is weak because it is only 1/3 of the total force, and most of it cancels at a distance.

**Electromagnetism:** The lattice medium can be displaced in two ways — outward (⊕) or inward (⊖). A particle's electric charge is determined by how its wave pattern winds between these two states. The strength of the electromagnetic force (the fine structure constant, α ≈ 1/137) comes from calculating how hard it is for a wave to tunnel through the lattice barriers. The calculation involves counting the number of independent tunneling paths on the cube — which turns out to be exactly 6 (these are called Gray codes on 3 bits). The result:

> α = 6^(−1/3) × exp(−barrier) = **1/137.036**

Every factor traces back to the geometry of the 3D cube. No free parameters.

**The Strong Force:** Three dimensions give three "colour" charges. At short range, the colour forces cancel perfectly (this is why protons do not explode). At longer range, the wave gets trapped inside its own container — that is confinement. The strong coupling constant α_s comes from the same lattice equation:

> α_s = 9/(8π²) × (1 + small correction) = **0.1179**

Observed: 0.1179. Exact match.

**The Weak Force:** When the medium slightly prefers one displacement direction over the other, left-handed and right-handed waves behave differently. That asymmetry is parity violation — it is not mysterious, it is just the medium having a slight preference.
